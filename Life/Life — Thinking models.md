## Real world problem solving

Interdisciplinary problems often require a combination of different approaches. Here's how one might apply these approaches:

**Mathematical Approach**: This can be used to create models of the problem. For example, if you're trying to optimize a process, you might use mathematical modeling to understand the relationships between different variables.

**Physics Approach**: This can be used to understand the underlying principles of the problem. For example, if you're designing a new material, you might need to understand the physical properties of the material.

**Engineering Approach**: This can be used to design and implement solutions. For example, if you're building a bridge, you would need to use engineering principles to ensure the bridge is safe and effective.

**Software Development Approach**: This can be used to automate processes and analyze data. For example, if you're analyzing large datasets, you might use software to automate the analysis and make it more efficient.

In all cases, the key is to understand the problem from multiple perspectives and to use the right approach for each aspect of the problem. It's also important to communicate effectively with experts from different fields and to integrate their knowledge and approaches into a cohesive solution. This requires not only technical knowledge, but also skills in collaboration and communication.  
  

## Interdiciplinary project

One example of a successful interdisciplinary project is the work done by the **Earth Institute**². They have tackled some of the most pressing sustainability issues in our world today, which requires more diversity of knowledge and methodology than a single discipline can provide².

For instance, they have worked on modeling future climate to adjust and prepare for agricultural vulnerabilities, and assessing vulnerability to winter storms across a city². These projects necessitate the crossing of boundaries between the economic, social, and technological disciplines to devise solutions².

Another example is the **SHAPE-ID project**, which is a major European Union-funded project championing interdisciplinarity¹. It provides tailored guidance for stakeholders interested in pursuing such partnerships, with a range of multimedia resources designed to guide you through the process¹.

These projects demonstrate how different disciplines can come together to address complex problems, leveraging the strengths of each field to create more comprehensive and effective solutions. They also highlight the importance of collaboration and communication in interdisciplinary work¹².
