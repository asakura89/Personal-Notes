Introduction
Evidence-Based Management (EBM) is a movement to use the best available evidence in management and decision-making. It aims to improve outcomes, reduce risks, and optimize investments in organizations. EBM involves critical thinking and scientific data to inform and inspire managerial actions and strategies. EBM draws on evidence from various disciplines, such as medicine, education, psychology, and sociology.
Steps of EBM
According to the Center for Evidence-Based Management (CEBMa), there are six steps involved in the EBM process:
- Asking: Translating a practical issue or problem into an answerable question.
- Acquiring: Systematically searching for and retrieving the evidence.
- Appraising: Critically judging the trustworthiness and relevance of the evidence.
- Aggregating: Weighing and pulling together the evidence.
- Applying: Incorporating the evidence into the decision-making process.
- Assessing: Evaluating the effectiveness of the decision.
Alternatively, managers can start using EBM in their organizations by employing five simple steps:
- Define the problem, question or issue.
- Gather internal evidence to check the relevance and validity of the matter.
- Collect and appraise external evidence regarding the issue.
- Consider views of stakeholders and those likely affected by the issue.
- Make a decision that integrates the above four sources of information.
Things to Know
EBM is based on three principles:
- Published peer-reviewed research evidence that bears on whether and why a particular management practice works.
- Judgment and experience from contextual management practice, to understand the organization and interpersonal dynamics in a situation and determine the risks and benefits of available actions.
- The preferences and values of those affected.
Practices indicative of an evidence-based organizational culture include:
- Systematic accumulation and analysis of organizational data.
- Problem-based reading and discussion of research summaries.
- Making decisions informed by best available research and organizational information.
Conclusion
In conclusion, Evidence-Based Management (EBM) is a powerful tool for managers to make informed decisions based on scientific data and critical thinking. By following the steps of EBM, managers can improve outcomes, reduce risks, and optimize investments in their organizations. It is important for managers to understand the principles of EBM and to cultivate an evidence-based organizational culture in order to fully realize its benefits.

- Plan your data collection carefully: To collect reliable and valid data, it is important to carefully plan your data collection process. This involves defining the problem or issue you want to address, selecting appropriate methods and techniques for collecting data, and ensuring that your data collection procedures are consistent and accurate.
- Use appropriate methods and techniques: The methods and techniques you use to collect data can have a significant impact on its reliability and validity. - Check for consistency: To ensure the reliability of your data, you should check for consistency across time, across different observers, and across parts of the test itself. This can help you identify any sources of error or bias in your data collection process.
- Verify accuracy: To ensure the validity of your data, you should verify its accuracy by checking how well it corresponds to established theories and other measures of the same concept. This can help you determine whether your data is measuring what it is intended to measure.
- Involve domain experts: One way to ensure both data reliability and validity is by exposing data to domain experts and business users, especially in small iterative phases. This can help you identify any errors or inaccuracies in your data and make any necessary corrections.
