# Team Lead

1. Leadership is an art form that can be learned, not taught.
2. Leadership involves embracing the idea of seeing what needs to be done and then exercising the art of getting people to want to join in and help.
3. Leaders take responsibility for the situation and take action, rather than being bystanders.
4. Leaders must be able to motivate and inspire others to join them in their cause.

### 10min summary of #160 TKP Insights: Leadership from The Knowledge Project with Shane Parrish

**1. Introduction**

Jim Collins examines how context-dependent good leadership is, and Jennifer Berger shares practical insights for great leadership. Randall Stutman from the Admired Leadership Institute talks about the importance of giving feedback and how it should be done correctly. The Knowledge Project podcast aims to master the best of what other people have already figured out by speaking with people at the top of their game.

This episode is sponsored by: Fizzle: Fizzle is an online business training platform that helps entrepreneurs start and grow their own businesses., FreshBooks: FreshBooks is an online accounting software designed to help small businesses and freelancers manage their finances., Skillshare: Skillshare is an online learning platform that offers courses in a variety of topics, including business, design, and technology., WP Engine: WP Engine is a managed WordPress hosting platform that provides hosting, security, and performance optimization for WordPress websites.

**Key takeaways:**

1. Leadership is context dependent and great leaders have practical insights that separate them from average leaders.
2. Giving feedback is essential, but how it is done is just as important.



**2. Leadership Strategies for Entrepreneurs and Professionals**

Cat Cole, president and COO of Athletic Greens, discussed the importance of doing right versus being right, the challenges of leading a business in different cultures, and a unique heuristic for accepting criticism. Alan Malali, former CEO of Ford, taught the elements of his working together management system that helps lead any team through principles and empowerment. Diana Chapman, co-founder of the Conscious Leadership Group, shared advice about running a team, the importance of unrelenting candor, and how teams can more effectively make decisions. The episode was sponsored by Metalab, a company that helps build products used by millions of people, and AeroPress, a coffee maker that makes a delicious, smooth, customizable cup of coffee in less than a minute.

**Key takeaways:**

1. Leaders should focus on doing right instead of being right, and use alan malali's working together management system to lead teams through principles and empowerment.
2. Diana chapman emphasizes the importance of unrelenting candor and effective decision making in teams.
3. Metalab and aeropress offer unique design and coffee making solutions for entrepreneurs and professionals.



**3. Learning the Art of Leadership**

A person can learn leadership, but it is not something that can be taught. It starts with recognizing something needs to be done and taking responsibility to get it done. Leadership is the art of getting people to want to do what must be done. It is an art form that is different for everyone, and people learn from each other rather than copying. It requires finding the right people to join in the effort and not being a bystander. It involves taking action and not just recognizing what needs to be done.

**Key takeaways:**

1. Leadership is an art form that can be learned, not taught.
2. Leadership involves embracing the idea of seeing what needs to be done and then exercising the art of getting people to want to join in and help.
3. Leaders take responsibility for the situation and take action, rather than being bystanders.
4. Leaders must be able to motivate and inspire others to join them in their cause.



**4. The Power of Presence**

Jennifer Garvey Burger states that the ability of a leader is largely dependent on how other people experience themselves in their presence. She cites the example of Eisenhower, who was successful in two very different contexts - military and presidential - but not as successful in his role as president of a university. She believes a great leader has a presence that makes other people bigger and can be seen in the way people physically respond to them - standing straighter, looking them in the eye, and smiling.

**Key takeaways:**

1. Leadership is highly contextual and can be successful in different contexts, as exemplified by eisenhower's successful leadership in both the military and presidential contexts.
2. Great leaders have a presence that makes other people bigger, allowing them to take risks and feel more comfortable to express themselves.



**5. The Qualities of a Successful Leader in the Current World**

A leader needs to have a real curiosity about the world, openness to other people's perspective, and the ability to amplify in order to be successful. This is different from leaders in the past who were often very certain about what was right and wrong and could predict the future. A curious leader is more likely to pick up on little signals that make a big difference before bad things happen. Political leaders may amplify, but they may not necessarily have the same qualities as a successful leader in the current world.

**Key takeaways:**

1. Leaders need to have a curiosity about the world and other people's perspectives in order to be successful.
2. Leaders need to be open to hearing when they are wrong and be able to pick up on signals that could lead to disaster.
3. Leaders need to be able to amplify their message and not be too certain about their own beliefs.



**6. Mastering the Art of Feedback**

Feedback is a difficult concept to master, as it is contextual and depends on the situation. Randall Stutman suggests that studying great leaders can provide insight into how to offer feedback, but that practical literature on the subject is often lacking in universal applicability. He further explains that the idea of asking permission to give feedback is a good idea in many cases, but that it doesn't work in all situations. Instead, he suggests building a routine of feedback that is tailored to the individual.

**Key takeaways:**

1. Leaders need to be mindful of the complexity of issues and take the time to understand them, rather than relying on soundbites.
2. Feedback is difficult to master and requires an understanding of the context in which it is given.
3. Routines are important in order to become a master of feedback, as they help to ensure that the right behavior is practiced in all situations.



**7. The Importance of Balanced Feedback**

Shane Parrish discussed the importance of providing feedback in balance. He explained that the best leaders start positive and go deep into the positive, matching the number and vividness of their criticisms. Parrish also suggested that leaders should stretch themselves to find more positives to focus on if they have many criticisms.

**Key takeaways:**

1. Leaders should strive to provide balanced feedback that is as vivid and detailed in its positives as it is in its negatives.
2. Leaders should start with the positives and go as deep as possible into them, and match the number of criticisms with positives.
3. Leaders should stretch themselves to find multiple positives to focus on.



**8. Adapting to Local Culture**

In Argentina, the franchisee had followed all the instructions given to them and had bought all the necessary kitchen equipment. However, the cooks were disgruntled as the menu offered an embarrassingly low quality ribeye steak sandwich cooked on a flat top grill, which was considered an abomination in Argentina. The choice of beef was deemed too low quality and the equipment was not up to the standards of the country. Shane Parrish had to make a decision whether to stick to the franchise standards or to listen to the feedback and make changes. He discussed this with the franchisee, who agreed that changes should be made. Despite the financial implications, Shane took the risk and made the changes to the menu and equipment to better suit the local culture.

**Key takeaways:**

1. Shane parrish realized the importance of local cultural knowledge when he encountered a problem in argentina with the hooters franchise. he took the feedback from the cooks and trainers and decided to make a change, despite the financial implications. he was honest about the struggle and the potential for the change to not be approved, but the franchisee appreciated his willingness to listen and act.



**9. Effective Management System for Success**

The event diagram consists of five elements: principles and practices, governance process, leadership team, creating value roadmap and business plan review. In order to have an effective operating system, the leader must be committed to certain characteristics and skills. Performance management, continuous improvement and lifelong learning are also important components. The strategy for the business should be laid out in the creating value roadmap, and the business plan should be reviewed regularly. The vision, strategy, plan, red flags and areas of focus should be discussed every week.

**Key takeaways:**

1. A successful management system requires a leader who is committed to certain character and skills.
2. The system should involve all stakeholders, and the leader should coach and facilitate rather than tell everyone what to do.
3. The system should also include a strategy for achieving the desired goals, as well as a business plan review process.
4. The leader should also be responsible for the work in the management system with zero tolerance for violating it, and should promote performance management and continuous improvement.



**10. Family Management System Implemented by Alan and Nicky Parrish**

Alan and Nicky Parrish have five children and have been married for 52 years. To help manage the family, Alan proposed a weekly family meeting agenda. The first agenda item is for each family member to pick up their own belongings and put them back in their room. Then, they take their laundry to the laundry room. With this system, everyone is held accountable for their own actions and must participate in order to keep the family running smoothly.

**Key takeaways:**

1. Alan and nicky parrish have implemented a working together management system in their family, which includes having a weekly family meeting and having everyone take responsibility for picking up their own stuff and doing their own laundry. this system helps to ensure that everyone is held accountable for their actions and that everyone is a leader in their own right.



**11. Encouraging Candor Through the "Fact and Story" Technique**

A family meeting was held every week in which the children would receive their allowances. The children enjoyed the family meeting, though they couldn't tell if it was out of enjoyment or obligation. All the children now run their own version of the family meeting. Diana Chapman believes that people don't practice enough candor and that people should be honest and open with each other. She encourages teams to tell each other their stories in order to learn from each other and grow. Diana suggests using the practice of fact and story, where each team member shares a fact and a story about someone else in the team. This helps the person relax and allows them to speak more candidly.

**Key takeaways:**

1. Teams should practice candor and be honest with each other in order to make better decisions.
2. One way to do this is by using the "fact and story" technique, which involves stating a fact and then sharing a story about it.
3. This helps team members relax and speak more candidly to one another.
