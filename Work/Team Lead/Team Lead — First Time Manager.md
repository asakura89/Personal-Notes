## Introduction

### Congratulations on being a manager

#### Introduction

Being a manager is a great opportunity to make a positive impact on the lives and careers of your team members. However, it also comes with a lot of responsibility and challenges. How can you become a great manager who inspires trust, respect, and loyalty from your team? How can you avoid the common pitfalls that can ruin your reputation and performance as a leader? In this essay, I will share with you some essential lessons that I learned from my experience as a senior executive and a business coach. These lessons will help you master both the management and the leadership aspects of your role, and enable you to communicate effectively with your team.

#### Management vs. Leadership

One of the first things you need to understand as a manager is the difference between management and leadership. Management is about planning, organizing, controlling, and measuring the work of your team. It involves setting goals, assigning tasks, monitoring progress, providing feedback, and solving problems. Leadership is about inspiring, motivating, empowering, and developing your team members. It involves creating a vision, building relationships, fostering collaboration, giving recognition, and facilitating growth.

Both management and leadership are important for your success as a manager. You need to balance them according to the situation and the needs of your team. Sometimes you need to focus more on management, such as when you have a tight deadline or a complex project. Other times you need to focus more on leadership, such as when you have a new team member or a low morale. The key is to be flexible and adaptable, and to use both management and leadership skills effectively.

#### Common Mistakes to Avoid

As a manager, you will inevitably make some mistakes along the way. That's normal and expected. However, there are some mistakes that can be very costly and damaging for your reputation and performance as a leader. You should avoid these mistakes at all costs. Here are some examples of common mistakes that managers make:
- <ins>Micromanaging:</ins> This is when you try to control every detail of your team's work, instead of giving them autonomy and trust. Micromanaging can demotivate your team, stifle their creativity, and lower their productivity. It can also make you look insecure and incompetent as a manager.
- <ins>Neglecting:</ins> This is when you ignore or disregard your team's needs, concerns, or feedback. Neglecting can make your team feel unappreciated, unsupported, and disengaged. It can also make you look indifferent and uncaring as a manager.
- <ins>Blaming:</ins> This is when you point fingers at your team or others for any mistakes or failures that happen under your watch. Blaming can erode your team's trust, respect, and loyalty. It can also make you look defensive and irresponsible as a manager.
- <ins>Avoiding:</ins> This is when you shy away from confronting or addressing any issues or conflicts that arise within or outside your team. Avoiding can escalate the problems, create confusion, and damage your credibility. It can also make you look weak and indecisive as a manager.

To avoid these mistakes, you need to adopt a positive and proactive attitude as a manager. You need to empower your team with clear expectations, guidance, and resources. You need to listen to your team with empathy, respect, and openness. You need to take responsibility for your team's results, both good and bad. And you need to face any challenges or difficulties with courage, honesty, and integrity.

#### Effective Communication Methods

Communication is one of the most important skills for any manager. Without communication, you cannot manage or lead effectively. You need to communicate with your team frequently, consistently, and clearly. You need to communicate with them in different ways and for different purposes. Here are some examples of effective communication methods that you can use as a manager:

- <ins>One-on-one meetings:</ins> These are regular meetings that you have with each of your team members individually. They are an opportunity for you to check in on their progress, provide feedback, offer support, address any issues, and discuss their goals and development.
- <ins>Team meetings:</ins> These are regular meetings that you have with your entire team collectively. They are an opportunity for you to share information, updates, announcements, or decisions that affect the whole team. They are also an opportunity for you to foster teamwork, collaboration, and alignment among your team members.
- <ins>Email:</ins> This is a written form of communication that you use to send or receive messages that are not urgent or time-sensitive. Email is useful for documenting information or instructions that need to be referred back later. However, email can also be misinterpreted or ignored easily if not written well.
- <ins>Phone call:</ins> This is a verbal form of communication that you use to talk to someone who is not physically present with you. Phone call is useful for conveying information or instructions that are urgent or time-sensitive. However, phone call can also be interrupted or distorted by noise or poor connection.
- <ins>Video call:</ins> This is a visual and verbal form of communication that you use to talk to someone who is not physically present with you. Video call is useful for having a more personal and engaging conversation with someone, especially if you need to see their facial expressions or body language. However, video call can also be affected by technical issues or distractions.

To communicate effectively with your team, you need to choose the right method for the right situation and the right purpose. You also need to follow some best practices for each method, such as:
- For one-on-one meetings, schedule them regularly, prepare an agenda, and take notes.
- For team meetings, have a clear objective, invite the relevant people, and keep them short and focused.
- For email, use a clear subject line, be concise and polite, and proofread before sending.
- For phone call, introduce yourself, speak clearly and slowly, and confirm understanding.
- For video call, test your equipment, find a quiet and well-lit place, and maintain eye contact.

#### Conclusion

Being a manager is not easy, but it can be very rewarding if you do it well. In this essay, I have shared with you some essential lessons that I learned from my experience as a senior executive and a business coach. These lessons will help you master both the management and the leadership aspects of your role, and enable you to communicate effectively with your team. By applying these lessons, you can become a great manager who inspires trust, respect, and loyalty from your team. You can also become someone's best manager ever. Congratulations on being a manager. I wish you all the best in your leadership journey.



### The rewards of leadership

#### Introduction

Leadership is a challenging but rewarding path that can bring you many benefits in your personal and professional life. However, it also comes with a lot of responsibility and power that you need to use wisely and ethically. In this essay, I will discuss some of the rewards of leadership that can motivate you to pursue and excel in your management role. These rewards include job security, earnings potential, positive impact, job satisfaction, and personal growth.

One of the rewards of leadership is that it can offer you more job security in an uncertain and competitive world. Another reward of leadership is that it can increase your earnings potential significantly. As a manager, you usually earn more than your team members, and you have more room for negotiation and advancement in your salary and compensation. Being a leader also means that you can demonstrate your value and contribution to your organization's success, and that you can leverage your network and reputation to access better opportunities and rewards.

A third reward of leadership is that it can enable you to make a positive impact on the lives and careers of your team members. As a manager, you have the opportunity to mentor, coach, support, and empower your team members to grow and succeed. #### Conclusion

Leadership is not easy, but it is worth it. In this essay, I have discussed some of the rewards of leadership that can motivate you to pursue and excel in your management role. These rewards include job security, earnings potential, positive impact, job satisfaction, and personal growth. By remembering these rewards, you can overcome the challenges and difficulties that come with leadership. You can also find your purpose and passion as a leader. Congratulations on being a manager. I hope you enjoy the rewards of leadership.



## 1. Understanding Your Responsibilities

### Management vs. leadership

#### Introduction

As a new manager, you might be wondering what your new role entails and how to perform it well. You might have some ideas or examples from your previous bosses, but not all of them might be helpful or relevant. One of the first things you need to learn as a new manager is the difference between management and leadership. These are two complementary but distinct skill sets that you need to master and balance in order to succeed as a manager. In this essay, I will explain what management and leadership are, how they differ, and how they work together.

#### Management

Management is the skill set that involves planning, organizing, controlling, and measuring the work of your team. It is about setting goals, assigning tasks, monitoring progress, providing feedback, and solving problems. Management is about getting the right things from people. It is about achieving measurable business outcomes and numbers. Management comes from your head. It requires logic, analysis, clarity, and accuracy.

#### Leadership

Leadership is the skill set that involves inspiring, motivating, empowering, and developing your team members. It is about creating a vision, building relationships, fostering collaboration, giving recognition, and facilitating growth. Leadership is about doing the right things for people. It is about creating a positive impact and culture for your team. Leadership comes from your heart. It requires empathy, trust, respect, and integrity.

#### The Difference

The difference between management and leadership can be summarized by a quote from Eleanor Roosevelt: "To handle yourself use your head, to handle others use your heart." Management is about handling yourself and your work effectively. Leadership is about handling others and their work effectively. Management is about what you do. Leadership is about who you are.

#### The Balance

The balance between management and leadership is essential for your success as a manager. You need both skill sets to work with your team effectively and efficiently. You need management skills to ensure that your team delivers quality work on time and on budget. You need leadership skills to ensure that your team feels valued, supported, and engaged in their work. You need to use both your head and your heart when working with your team.

#### Conclusion

As a new manager, you have a great opportunity to learn and grow as a professional and as a person. One of the key lessons you need to learn is the difference between management and leadership. These are two complementary but distinct skill sets that you need to master and balance in order to succeed as a manager. Management is about getting the right things from people. Leadership is about doing the right things for people. Management comes from your head. Leadership comes from your heart. By applying both skill sets in your work, you can become an effective and respected manager.



### Be an example

#### Introduction

As a manager, you have a great influence on your team's behavior and performance. Your team will look up to you and follow your lead, just like ducklings follow their mother duck. Therefore, you need to be an example of the kind of behavior that you want your team to exhibit. This is inspirational leadership, and it is based on three pillars: integrity, emotional intelligence, and accountability. In this essay, I will explain what each pillar means and how you can apply it in your management style.

#### Integrity

Integrity is the pillar of inspirational leadership that involves being consistent and transparent with your principles and actions. It means that you practice what you preach, and that you align your words and deeds. For example, if you value punctuality, you should always be on time for meetings and deadlines. If you value open communication, you should always listen attentively and respectfully to feedback and criticism. Integrity is important for inspirational leadership because it builds trust, respect, and credibility with your team. Your team will see you as a reliable and honest leader, and they will be more likely to follow your example.

#### Emotional Intelligence

Emotional intelligence is the pillar of inspirational leadership that involves being aware and in control of your own emotions, and being empathetic and responsive to the emotions of others. It means that you can manage your emotional reactions in different situations, and that you can understand and relate to the feelings and perspectives of your team members. For example, if you are stressed or angry, you should not lash out or vent at your team. Instead, you should calm yourself down and express your emotions constructively. If your team member is frustrated or sad, you should not ignore or dismiss their feelings. Instead, you should acknowledge and support them emotionally. Emotional intelligence is important for inspirational leadership because it enhances communication, collaboration, and motivation with your team. Your team will see you as a compassionate and respectful leader, and they will be more likely to emulate your behavior.

#### Accountability

Accountability is the pillar of inspirational leadership that involves taking responsibility for your own mistakes and your team's mistakes. It means that you do not blame others or make excuses for any failures or errors that happen under your watch. Instead, you own up to them and learn from them. For example, if your team member does something wrong, you should not point fingers or criticize them harshly. Instead, you should admit your role in the mistake and help them correct it. If you do something wrong yourself, you should not hide or deny it. Instead, you should apologize and fix it. Accountability is important for inspirational leadership because it fosters learning, improvement, and team culture with your team. Your team will see you as a humble and responsible leader, and they will be more likely to follow your example.

#### Conclusion

As a manager, you have a great opportunity to inspire your team with your behavior and performance. Your team will look up to you and follow your lead, just like ducklings follow their mother duck. Therefore, you need to be an example of the kind of behavior that you want your team to exhibit. This is inspirational leadership, and it is based on three pillars: integrity, emotional intelligence, and accountability. By applying these pillars in your management style, you can become an effective and respected leader.



### Expectations and check-ins

#### Introduction

As a manager, you want to avoid giving your team members unexpected or unpleasant feedback that can demoralize or frustrate them. You also want to ensure that your team members deliver quality work that meets your standards and expectations. How can you achieve these goals? The answer is to set clear expectations and check in frequently with your team members. In this essay, I will explain why these two practices are important and how you can implement them in your management style.

#### Setting Clear Expectations

Setting clear expectations is the practice of communicating to your team members what you want them to do, how you want them to do it, and how you will evaluate their work. It is not enough to just give them the task and the deadline. You also need to give them the criteria and the indicators that will determine their success or failure. For example, if you assign your team member to write an article for the company website, you should not only tell them the topic and the due date, but also the word count, the tone, the style, the format, and the tools that you will use to check their spelling, grammar, and SEO. Setting clear expectations is important because it helps your team members understand your requirements and standards, and it gives them a clear direction and guidance for their work. It also helps you avoid misunderstandings, confusion, or disappointment later on when you review their work.

#### Checking In Frequently

Checking in frequently is the practice of monitoring and reviewing your team members' progress and performance on a regular basis. It does not mean micromanaging or controlling every detail of their work. It means giving them feedback, support, and recognition along the way. You can check in with your team members through different methods, such as email, phone call, video call, or face-to-face meeting. You can also ask them how they prefer to communicate with you and how often they want you to check in with them. Checking in frequently is important because it helps you catch any problems or errors early on and correct them before they become bigger or worse. It also helps you keep track of your team members' achievements and challenges, and it gives them an opportunity to ask questions, share ideas, or express concerns.

#### Conclusion

As a manager, you have a responsibility to give your team members constructive and timely feedback that can help them improve their work and performance. You also have a responsibility to ensure that your team members deliver quality work that meets your expectations and standards. You can achieve these responsibilities by setting clear expectations and checking in frequently with your team members. These two practices will help you avoid unexpected or unpleasant feedback, and they will help you build trust, respect, and collaboration with your team.



### Make it safe

#### Introduction

As a manager, you want your team members to feel comfortable and confident to share their ideas, opinions, and feedback with you. You also want them to be willing and able to take risks, innovate, and grow. However, this can only happen if you create a safe work environment for your team. A safe work environment is one where your team members feel respected, supported, and trusted by you and by each other. In this essay, I will discuss how you can create a safe work environment for your team using a three-pronged approach: encouraging honest feedback, allowing smart risk-taking, and maintaining consistency.

#### Encouraging Honest Feedback

Encouraging honest feedback is the first prong of creating a safe work environment for your team. Honest feedback is essential for improving your team's performance, communication, and collaboration. However, your team members might be reluctant or afraid to share their feedback with you or with each other if they fear negative consequences, such as criticism, rejection, or punishment. Therefore, you need to make it clear and easy for your team members to speak their minds without fear. Here are some ways you can do that:
- Keep an open mind. Listen to your team members' feedback with curiosity and appreciation, not with defensiveness or judgment. Don't react negatively or dismissively to their feedback, even if you disagree or don't like it. Instead, thank them for their input and ask questions to understand their perspective.
- Invite feedback. Don't assume that your team members will come to you with their feedback just because you have an open-door policy or a friendly attitude. Actively invite them to share their thoughts and opinions with you on a regular basis. You can do this by asking open-ended questions, soliciting feedback in meetings, or conducting surveys or polls.
- Give trust. Trust is the foundation of honest feedback. Your team members will be more likely to trust you with their feedback if you trust them first. You can show your trust by delegating tasks, empowering decisions, sharing information, and giving recognition.
- Support feedback. Support your team members when they share their feedback in meetings or in front of others. You don't have to agree with everything they say, but you should acknowledge and respect their contribution. You should also protect them from any backlash or retaliation from others who might not appreciate their feedback.

#### Allowing Smart Risk-Taking

Allowing smart risk-taking is the second prong of creating a safe work environment for your team. Smart risk-taking is the practice of trying new things, experimenting with different approaches, and learning from failures. It is necessary for innovation, growth, and excellence. However, your team members might be hesitant or afraid to take risks if they worry about the consequences of failure, such as blame, embarrassment, or loss of reputation. Therefore, you need to make it okay for your team members to take risks and fail without fear. Here are some ways you can do that:
- Encourage risks. Encourage your team members to step out of their comfort zone and try new things that can benefit the team or the organization. Don't limit them to the status quo or the proven methods. Instead, challenge them to explore new possibilities and opportunities.
- Coach risks. Coach your team members on how to take smart risks that are aligned with the team's goals and values. Help them assess the potential benefits and costs of their risks, and plan for contingencies and mitigation strategies. Don't micromanage or interfere with their risks, but offer guidance and support when needed.
- Reward risks. Reward your team members for taking smart risks, regardless of the outcome. Don't punish or criticize them for failing or making mistakes, but celebrate their effort and learning. Give them constructive feedback on how they can improve their risk-taking skills and outcomes in the future.

#### Maintaining Consistency

Maintaining consistency is the third prong of creating a safe work environment for your team. Consistency is the practice of being reliable and predictable in your behavior and actions as a manager. It means that you follow through on your promises and commitments, and that you treat your team members fairly and equitably. Consistency is important for creating a safe work environment because it builds trust, respect, and credibility with your team. Your team members will feel more secure and confident in working with you if they know what to expect from you and how you will treat them. Here are some ways you can maintain consistency as a manager:
- Share your values and principles. Share with your team the values and principles that guide your management style and decisions. Explain why they are important to you and how they affect your work and behavior. Be transparent and honest about your expectations and standards for yourself and for your team.
- Act with integrity. Act in accordance with your values and principles at all times. Don't compromise or violate them for any reason. Don't say one thing and do another. Don't change your mind or your rules without a good reason. Be consistent and coherent in your words and deeds.
- Treat your team members equitably. Treat your team members with the same level of respect, care, and professionalism, regardless of their performance, personality, or background. Don't play favorites or discriminate against anyone. Don't let your emotions or biases influence your treatment of your team members.

#### Conclusion

As a manager, you have a responsibility to create a safe work environment for your team. A safe work environment is one where your team members feel respected, supported, and trusted by you and by each other. You can create a safe work environment for your team using a three-pronged approach: encouraging honest feedback, allowing smart risk-taking, and maintaining consistency. By applying these practices in your management style, you can foster a positive and productive team culture.



### Create career paths

#### Introduction

As a manager, you want your team members to feel motivated, engaged, and fulfilled in their work. You also want them to stay loyal and committed to your organization. However, this can only happen if you create career paths for each of your team members. A career path is a plan that outlines the possible opportunities and steps for your team members to advance and grow in their careers. It is not just a job, but a journey. In this essay, I will discuss why it is important to create career paths for your team members, and how it can benefit them and you as a manager.

#### Benefits of Creating Career Paths

Creating career paths for your team members can bring you many benefits as a manager. Here are some of them:
- Retention: Creating career paths can help you retain your team members by showing them that you care about their future and that you have opportunities for them to progress and succeed in your organization. Don't check your devices or look away from them. Don't think about what you are going to say next or how you are going to respond. Instead, focus on their words, tone, body language, and emotions. ### Content, emotion, and intent

#### Introduction

As a manager, you want your team members to trust, respect, and communicate with you effectively. You also want to understand their needs, concerns, and feedback better. However, this can only happen if you become a great listener. A great listener is not just someone who pays attention to what is being said, but also someone who makes the speaker feel heard and understood. In this essay, I will discuss the three things you need to do to be a great listener: listening for content, listening for intent, and listening for emotion.

#### Listening for Content

Listening for content is the first thing you need to do to be a great listener. It means that you focus on the details and facts of what the speaker is saying, and that you can recall and summarize them accurately. Listening for content is important because it shows that you are interested and attentive to the speaker's message, and that you can follow their logic and arguments. Here are some ways you can listen for content effectively:
- Ask clarifying questions. Ask questions that help you understand the speaker's message better, such as "What do you mean by that?" or "Can you give me an example?" Don't ask questions that interrupt or challenge the speaker, such as "Why do you think that?" or "Don't you think that's wrong?"
- Paraphrase or repeat what you heard. Paraphrase or repeat what the speaker said in your own words to show that you understood them correctly, such as "So what you're saying is..." or "Let me see if I got this right..." Don't change or distort the speaker's message, such as "So what you really mean is..." or "You're basically saying that..."
- Take notes. Take notes of the key points and information that the speaker is sharing with you, especially if the message is complex or lengthy. Don't write down everything word for word, but focus on the main ideas and facts. Don't let your note-taking distract you from listening or making eye contact with the speaker.

#### Listening for Intent

Listening for intent is the second thing you need to do to be a great listener. It means that you try to understand the purpose and goal of the speaker's message, and what they want to achieve or get from the conversation. Listening for intent is important because it helps you grasp the underlying meaning and motivation of the speaker's message, and how it relates to their values and interests. Here are some ways you can listen for intent effectively:
- Use the why question. Use the why question to probe deeper into the speaker's message and uncover their intent, such as "Why are you telling me this?" or "Why do you think this is important?" Don't use the why question to accuse or criticize the speaker, such as "Why did you do that?" or "Why don't you listen?"
- Look for clues. Look for clues that indicate the speaker's intent in their words, tone, body language, and emotions. For example, if the speaker uses words like "need", "want", "must", or "should", they might be expressing a desire, a demand, a necessity, or an obligation. If the speaker uses a confident or assertive tone, they might be trying to persuade or convince you of something. If the speaker uses gestures like pointing, nodding, or shaking their head, they might be emphasizing or agreeing or disagreeing with something. If the speaker shows emotions like happiness, anger, sadness, or fear, they might be revealing their feelings or attitudes towards something.
- Reflect back what you heard. Reflect back what you heard from the speaker in terms of their intent, such as "It sounds like you want me to..." or "It seems like you are trying to..." Don't assume or judge the speaker's intent, but check with them if your interpretation is correct.

#### Listening for Emotion

Listening for emotion is the third thing you need to do to be a great listener. It means that you pay attention to and empathize with the feelings and emotions of the speaker, and how they affect their message and behavior. Listening for emotion is important because it shows that you care about and respect the speaker as a person, not just as a source of information. It also helps you build rapport and trust with the speaker, and diffuse any potential conflicts or tensions. Here are some ways you can listen for emotion effectively:
- Identify the emotion. Identify the emotion that the speaker is expressing or experiencing in their message, such as happiness, anger, sadness, fear, surprise, or disgust. You can use your own emotional intelligence and awareness to recognize and label the emotion accurately.
- Validate the emotion. Validate the emotion that the speaker is expressing or experiencing by acknowledging and accepting it as legitimate and understandable, such as "I can see why you are happy/angry/sad/fearful/surprised/disgusted." Don't invalidate or dismiss the emotion by ignoring or rejecting it as irrelevant or unreasonable, such as "You shouldn't be happy/angry/sad/fearful/surprised/disgusted."
- Respond to the emotion. Respond to the emotion that the speaker is expressing or experiencing by showing empathy and support, such as "I'm happy/sorry/concerned for you." or "How can I help you?" Don't respond to the emotion by showing indifference or hostility, such as "So what?" or "That's your problem."

#### Conclusion

As a manager, you want to be a great listener who makes your team members and others feel heard and understood. A great listener is not just someone who pays attention to what is being said, but also someone who listens for content, intent, and emotion. Listening for content means that you focus on the details and facts of what the speaker is saying, and that you can recall and summarize them accurately. Listening for intent means that you try to understand the purpose and goal of the speaker's message, and what they want to achieve or get from the conversation. Listening for emotion means that you pay attention to and empathize with the feelings and emotions of the speaker, and how they affect their message and behavior. By listening for content, intent, and emotion, you can become a great listener who can communicate, collaborate, and relate with your team members and others effectively.



### Leverage two-way communication

#### Introduction

As a manager, you want your team members to understand and execute the tasks that you delegate to them effectively. You also want to avoid any misunderstandings, confusion, or errors that can result from poor communication. However, this can only happen if you leverage two-way communication. Two-way communication is the practice of exchanging feedback and confirmation between the sender and the receiver of a message. It is not just a one-way transmission, but a dialogue. In this essay, I will discuss three techniques for using two-way communication to ensure that you and your team are on the same page: getting confirmation, modeling confirmation, and follow-up confirmation.

#### Getting Confirmation

Getting confirmation is the first technique for using two-way communication to ensure that you and your team are on the same page. It means that you ask your team members to repeat or summarize what they heard from you after you give them a message, such as a task, an instruction, or a request. Getting confirmation is important because it helps you verify that your message was received and understood correctly by your team members, and that they know what they need to do and why. Here are some ways you can get confirmation effectively:
- Ask politely and respectfully. Ask your team members to confirm what they heard from you in a polite and respectful way, such as "Would you please confirm back to me what you heard me say? I'd like to be sure I'm communicating clearly." Don't make it sound like a test or a challenge, such as "Do you understand?" or "Did you get that?"
- Focus on your communication, not their listening. Focus on your communication skills and responsibility, not on their listening skills or ability. Don't blame or criticize them for not hearing or understanding you correctly, but acknowledge that communication can be difficult or complex sometimes. Don't say "You didn't listen" or "You misunderstood", but say "I might not have explained it well" or "I might have used some unclear terms."
- Give them time and space. Give your team members enough time and space to process and recall what they heard from you, and to formulate their response. Don't rush them or interrupt them while they are confirming what they heard from you. Don't correct them or fill in the gaps for them while they are speaking. Let them finish their confirmation before you give them any feedback or clarification.

#### Modeling Confirmation

Modeling confirmation is the second technique for using two-way communication to ensure that you and your team are on the same page. It means that you repeat or summarize what you heard from your team members after they give you a message, such as an idea, an opinion, or a feedback. Modeling confirmation is important because it shows that you are interested and attentive to your team members' message, and that you respect and value their input. It also helps you set an example and encourage your team members to use two-way communication as well. Here are some ways you can model confirmation effectively:
- Ask for permission and confirmation. Ask for permission and confirmation from your team members before you repeat or summarize what you heard from them, such as "Would it be okay if I check in and make sure I'm hearing you correctly?" or "Did I get that right?" Don't assume or impose that you understood them correctly, but check with them if your interpretation is accurate.
- Use your own words and tone. Use your own words and tone when you repeat or summarize what you heard from your team members, rather than mimicking or parroting their words and tone. Don't change or distort their message, but rephrase it in a way that makes sense to you. Don't mock or ridicule their message, but express it in a respectful and sincere way.
- Show empathy and support. Show empathy and support for your team members when you repeat or summarize what you heard from them, especially if they are expressing or experiencing any emotions or feelings in their message, such as happiness, anger, sadness, or fear. Don't ignore or dismiss their emotions or feelings, but acknowledge and validate them as legitimate and understandable. Don't respond with indifference or hostility, but with compassion and care.

#### Follow-Up Confirmation

Follow-up confirmation is the third technique for using two-way communication to ensure that you and your team are on the same page. It means that you ask your team members to send you a written summary or notes of your conversation or meeting after it ends, especially if it was long or detailed. Follow-up confirmation is important because it helps you document and review what was discussed and agreed upon in your conversation or meeting, and it helps your team members remember and reinforce what they heard from you. It also helps you track and monitor their progress and performance on the tasks or actions that were assigned or decided in your conversation or meeting. Here are some ways you can request follow-up confirmation effectively:
- Explain the purpose and the benefits. Explain the purpose and the benefits of follow-up confirmation to your team members, such as "I'd like you to send me a summary or notes of what we discussed and any action steps identified. This will help us both remember and review what we agreed on, and it will help us keep track of our progress and performance."
- Specify the format and the deadline. Specify the format and the deadline of follow-up confirmation to your team members, such as "Please send me an email or a document with a bullet-point list of the key points and information that we covered, and the tasks or actions that we assigned or decided. Please do this by the end of the day or by tomorrow morning."
- Provide feedback and appreciation. Provide feedback and appreciation to your team members when they send you their follow-up confirmation, such as "Thank you for sending me your summary or notes. I appreciate your effort and attention. I have reviewed them and they are accurate and clear. I have no further questions or comments at this point."

#### Conclusion

As a manager, you want to be a great listener who makes your team members feel heard and understood. A great listener is not just someone who pays attention to what is being said, but also someone who uses two-way communication to ensure that you and your team are on the same page. Two-way communication is the practice of exchanging feedback and confirmation between the sender and the receiver of a message. It is not just a one-way transmission, but a dialogue. You can use two-way communication to ensure that you and your team are on the same page by getting confirmation, modeling confirmation, and requesting follow-up confirmation. By using these techniques, you can improve your communication, collaboration, and relationship with your team members.

#### The problem with blame

Blame is a negative and destructive leadership mindset that can have serious consequences for your team's performance and morale. Blame is when you point the finger at someone or something else for the mistakes or failures that happen in your team. Blame can manifest in different ways, such as:
- Accusing your team members of being incompetent, lazy, or dishonest
- Complaining about the lack of resources, support, or time
- Making excuses or rationalizations for the poor results or outcomes
- Denying or avoiding your own role or contribution to the problem

Blame has several problems as a leadership mindset. First, blame immediately puts people on the defensive. #### The solution of ownership

Ownership is a positive and constructive leadership mindset that can have significant benefits for your team's performance and morale. - Offering feedback or solutions to the problem
- Taking action to fix the problem or prevent it from happening again
- Learning from the mistakes or failures and applying the lessons to future situations

Ownership has several advantages as a leadership mindset. First, ownership creates a culture of trust and respect in your team. When you take responsibility for everything that happens in your team, you show your team members that you care about them and their work. Be clear about the goals and priorities. Be consistent in your words and actions. Be fair in your decisions and evaluations. Be supportive in your coaching and feedback.
- When creating a work environment for your team members, be mindful of their needs and preferences. Make sure they have access to the resources, support, and time they need to do their work effectively and comfortably. Also make sure they have opportunities to interact with each other and with you in formal and informal settings. Encourage them to share their ideas, opinions, and concerns with you and with each other.
- When dealing with mistakes or failures in your team, be aware of them and address them promptly. Don't ignore or avoid them. Don't blame or accuse anyone. Don't make excuses or rationalizations. Instead, acknowledge your own role or contribution to the problem. Apologize for the mistakes or failures that happen in your team. Seek feedback or input from your team members or stakeholders. Offer feedback or solutions to the problem. Take action to fix the problem or prevent it from happening again. Learn from the mistakes or failures and apply the lessons to future situations.

By practicing ownership in your team, you can create a positive and productive work culture where your team members can thrive and succeed.



#### Conclusion

Blame is a negative and destructive leadership mindset that can harm your team's performance and morale. Blame is when you point the finger at someone or something else for the mistakes or failures that happen in your team. Blame puts people on the defensive, focuses on what went wrong, and creates a culture of fear and finger-pointing.

Ownership is a positive and constructive leadership mindset that can benefit your team's performance and morale. Ownership is when you take responsibility for everything that happens in your team and look for ways to improve and learn from the errors. Ownership creates a culture of trust and respect, fosters a culture of learning and improvement, and inspires a culture of engagement and commitment.

As a leader, you should avoid blame and adopt ownership in your team. You should also practice ownership in your team by hiring people who have an ownership mindset, communicating with them respectfully and honestly, creating a work environment that meets their needs and preferences, and dealing with mistakes or failures promptly and constructively.

By doing this, you can create a team that is more productive, innovative, resilient, and satisfied. You can also create a team that is more fun and rewarding to work with.



## 6. Put yourself in their shoes: How to develop your personal leadership style

### The golden rule of leadership

One of the most important principles of leadership is to follow the golden rule: "Do unto others as you would have them do unto you." This means treating your team members with respect, empathy, and kindness. It also means providing them with the support, feedback, and recognition they need to succeed.

A good way to apply the golden rule is to think back to your own experiences as a team member. Think about your current manager and all the managers you have had before. What did they do right? What did they do wrong? How did they make you feel? What did you learn from them?

By reflecting on your own experiences, you can identify the best practices and the worst practices of leadership. You can emulate the things you loved about your managers' style and avoid the things you hated. You can also fill in the gaps by offering the things you wish your managers had done for you.

By doing this, you can build your own personal leadership style by being the leader you wish you had.
