**Section 1: Optimizing the Project Schedule**

We're nearly finished with planning your first project. The last step is to optimize the schedule. Start by ensuring that all project work is included in the schedule. Remember, you might have created the Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) with only 80% of the project requirements documented. Now, it's time to go back and ensure that all documented requirements have corresponding tasks in the schedule. 

This includes items on the Release Readiness Checklist and your risk management plans. It's not unusual for some work packages or risk plans to be unclear. For example, the technical lead might be waiting for final feedback from the security architect, which could result in a new requirement. For these unclear tasks, add a placeholder task to the schedule with a conservative work estimate. These placeholder tasks are actually schedule buffers, which we'll discuss more later. 

In terms of risk management, you might not know if a risk has occurred, so you need to add tasks to check whether the risk is active. For example, "Check the weather forecast before committing to the foundation pour." If bad weather is expected and pouring the foundation will be delayed, the next task in the workflow is a weather buffer task which is activated. If there's no expected delay, that task can be marked as completed with zero duration. Once you're sure that all known work is captured, it's time to move to the next step.

**Section 2: Accounting for Non-Working Days**

After verifying that all work is represented, ensure that all holidays and non-working days are included in the scheduling tool. This includes team members' planned vacations, training, etc. It can be difficult to get your teammates to provide their vacation plans, especially if you're asking in February and they haven't started thinking about their summer vacation. In those cases, add a placeholder for any vacation or training time that will last longer than a few days. 

These non-working days are tracked in the calendar function of your scheduling application and are not specific tasks in the schedule. It's wise to plan for those times when your team members will not be working on your project to avoid having them work overtime to catch up on the project work once they return. If the project will span end-of-year holidays such as Thanksgiving, Christmas, and New Year's, block the full week for each holiday as non-working days. Do the same for Chinese New Year if needed. This provides a little extra buffer for the schedule and accounts for the fact that much of the team will be taking time off.

**Section 1: Adding Buffers to the Schedule**

Now it's time to add more buffers. For each major work package, add a buffer task to the schedule to prepare for unexpected issues we discussed earlier. Place these buffer tasks right before the completion milestone and make it a predecessor for the next task in the workflow. Don't worry about how much work to assign to each buffer yet; just add them into the schedule between major work packages or milestones. 

I usually add buffers to the development work, the validation work, and a general buffer right before the project release milestone. To figure out how much buffer you need, look at the total estimated work and calculate a percentage of the total to distribute across the project timeline. For projects where I feel we have high confidence in the requirements and low to moderate risk, I typically use a buffer of 15%; for projects with many unknowns and higher risk, I use 25%. If you're not sure, start with 20%. 

This buffered schedule is your "worst-case" scenario, which means that even if things go slightly wrong and the team encounters some obstacles you didn't plan for, you can still meet the buffered release commitment. As long as nothing unexpected and significantly impactful happens, your team can deliver to this schedule; it's your "high-confidence" commit. The flip side is also true; even if things go very well, the likelihood of the team delivering the project much earlier than this buffered date is low. You and your management chain may not like this date, but it is the most likely date the team can deliver to unless specific actions are taken to pull in the schedule.

**Section 2: The Importance of Buffering a Schedule**

Yes, a buffered schedule provides a high-confidence commitment to deliver, but is that realistic? Many organizations view schedule buffers as unnecessary padding and scrutinize any project schedule in an attempt to eliminate buffers. This is extremely short-sighted in my opinion, because you are then planning for nothing to go wrong, for the sun to always shine, and the design to always work. That's just not realistic, is it? 

Instead, I see buffers as part of responsible project planning. A well-buffered schedule significantly increases the predictability of delivering the project on time, and isn't that the point of planning in the first place? High predictability of project execution allows an organization to better utilize resources and coordinate work across programs. Furthermore, a buffered schedule ensures that the team isn't burnt out at the end because delays and unexpected risks were planned for.

**Section 1: Balancing Stakeholder Expectations and Project Realities**

You might feel stuck between the expectations of stakeholders and the high-confidence schedule. It's crucial to understand that stakeholder expectations are mainly based on business needs and possibly a commitment to a customer. They are not based on the actual work needed to produce the project deliverables. The high-confidence schedule is firmly based on the actual work content and may not meet that business need. Now, it's time to optimize the schedule to see if it's possible to bridge the gap between the stakeholders' expectations and reality.

The first step when adjusting a schedule is to look for ways to optimize the workflow. This is the easy part. Identify work that can be done simultaneously and reorder the workflow to shorten the overall project duration. Look for specific tasks that can start earlier with a partial predecessor deliverable. Any work you can move off of the critical path will shorten the overall project duration, so carefully examine those tasks and see if there are opportunities to reorder the work. Once you've done all of the workflow optimization you can, baseline your schedule and make a copy. You will use this copy to do "what-if" scenario modeling next.

**Section 2: Adjusting the Project Schedule**

In Chapter 2, we discussed the Triple Constraint and the fact that you must balance the project's schedule, scope, and resources. There really are only two ways to adjust your project schedule—either reduce the scope of work or add more resources—so let's dig a little deeper into what that looks like.

Reducing the scope of work is often the most realistic way to adjust a schedule since resources don't grow on trees. As a team, carefully examine the requirements and identify any that are considered "nice to haves" or that can be delayed. Consider using a phased approach to implementing requirements that will allow the team to deliver the most important functionality on time. 

Another completely viable, though unpopular, strategy is to reduce the quality of the project deliverable through reducing the amount of validation to be performed, so that again, the most important functionality can be delivered on time. Use that copy of the schedule you made to do "what-if" scenario modeling by cutting tasks from the schedule to understand the impact on the overall timeline. Be sure to keep track of the schedule impact each of these reduced-scope options generates.

**Section 1: Crashing the Schedule and Negotiating with Stakeholders**

Adding resources to the schedule is humorously referred to as "crashing the schedule." Often, less experienced project managers make a general request for more resources, such as "We need five more resources to release this product by the end of October." However, this request is too vague and not actionable for stakeholders. The key to getting more resources is to identify specific work packages where they can be applied to speed up the schedule. 

To do this, you use your "what-if" scenario modeling schedule and add resources to tasks on the critical path to see the impact on the final date. By doing this modeling, you and your team can identify some strategic work packages where additional resources can have a significant impact. Now, the request looks something like this: "If we can get an additional database analyst for the first two weeks of June, then we can pull the schedule in by three weeks." Now, the stakeholder who can provide that resource knows exactly what you need and when you need it, and is more likely to act.

At this point, you should have your high-confidence schedule and several options for pulling it in, so now it's time to negotiate with your stakeholders. This negotiation is often contentious and difficult because the project manager hasn't done their homework. The job of the project manager here is to provide viable options to the decision-makers. We get paid whether we execute Plan A or Plan B, but it's our responsibility to provide the data to the decision-makers so that they can make informed decisions about additional resources or reduced scope.

**Section 2: Finalizing the Project Plan**

The careful planning you and your team have done so far has generated that data through the "what-if" scenario modeling, and while your stakeholders may huff and puff a bit, eventually they will face reality and make some decisions that allow you to lock in the plan and schedule. At this point, you can obtain stakeholder buy-off on the major project milestones, so update your baseline schedule with any changes based on these negotiations. Re-baseline the schedule and use these dates as your commitment to deliver. Lastly, send out a formal communication committing the project to these new dates.

That's it! You are finished planning your project! The Planning Phase is where most of the mechanics of project management happen, and it's all downhill from here with respect to artifacts you need to generate or specific project management processes you need to follow.
