# Manager

## What is

Manager ini orang yang me-manage dirinya sendiri, me-manage sekelompok orang, me-manage project, dan me-manage proses-proses di dalemnya. Biasanya Manager gak berurusan sama hal-hal yang ada di lapangan. Atau berurusan langsung dengan tugas-tugas lapangan. Atau gampangnya Manager ini bukan seorang executor, tapi monitor.

Yang lain juga, biasanya Manager ini tugas-tugasnya mengurusi hal yang disebut strategic tasks. Ngurusin pengembangan team. Pertumbuhan individual dari team-nya. Goal-goal perusahaan. Dan yang paling penting, Menager juga adalah orang yang ngebersihin kerikil-kerikil atau roadblocks dalam perjalanan project dan team.

Dan selain task managerial di atas, Manager juga harus udah punya dan ahli dalam [skill-skill yang dimiliki seorang IC](Work%20%E2%80%94%20Individual%20Contributor.md).

## What Makes a Great Software Engineer Manager

Section 1:
The paper presents a study exploring the attributes of great software engineering managers. 37 software engineers and managers at Microsoft were interviewed to identify important attributes. The attributes were organized into a framework with two dimensions: manager interactions at the individual or team level, and three key functions of managers - cultivating engineering wisdom, motivating engineers, and mediating communication. 

15 attributes emerged: being available, being technical, enabling autonomy, supporting experimentation, growing talent, promoting fairness, building relationships, recognizing individuality, clearing execution path, building team culture, guiding the team, maintaining positive environment, inspiring the team, facilitating communication, and driving alignment. 

Being technical involves comprehending the system, technologies and solutions to facilitate discussions, arbitrate dilemmas, and mentor engineers. However, people management skills were seen as most critical, not technical excellence. A survey of 3,646 engineers and managers ranked the attributes. Maintaining a positive environment, growing talent and enabling autonomy were most important. Being technical ranked 14th.

Section 2: 

The qualitative data showed why attributes are important and how they manifest. Enabling autonomy gives engineers freedom but managers provide context for decisions. Supporting experimentation encourages skill growth but managers ensure quality. Growing talent involves providing challenges, feedback and on-the-job learning. Promoting fairness means showing appreciation publicly and correcting privately. Recognizing individuality helps match work to skills and interests. Clearing execution path removes distractions so engineers can focus. Building team culture establishes quality norms. Guiding the team means coaching on quality while leaving implementation to engineers. Maintaining a positive environment provides work-life balance and celebrates successes. Inspiring the team makes managers leaders, not just managers. Facilitating communication means acting as a buffer with other teams and negotiating workload. Driving alignment shares strategic context so effort is channeled appropriately.  

Section 3:

The survey found engineers and managers aligned overall but managers valued team relationships, inspiring and growing talent more. Managers strongly favored hiring social over technical managers. Females valued being technical, inspiring, guiding and growing talent more. Larger teams valued autonomy less. Longer tenured employees valued growing talent less. Compared to other knowledge workers, software engineering valued building team culture and driving alignment less but clearing execution path more, reflecting the importance of maintaining focus.

In summary, the framework offers actionable practices on cultivating, motivating and enabling communication for managers. People skills are critical, not just technical skills. The contextualized attributes can inform research on measuring management impact and tailoring training. Similarities to management psychology builds validity while differences highlight software engineering needs.

Section 4: 

The authors discuss implications for research and practice. The attributes and strategies can be studied to measure impact on productivity, quality, and other engineering outcomes. Similarities to concepts like psychological empowerment and perceived organizational fit indicate theories to test. Differing perceptions between roles over time could inform training. 

For practice, the framework guides engineering managers on important attributes. Technical excellence is less critical than people skills, informing selection and training. Managers should educate themselves on management psychology theories that influence software engineering. Tailored management training is needed as software engineering values some attributes less than other domains.

Section 5:

The limitations are considered. The meaning of a "great manager" is subjective. Interviewees were purposely sampled for diversity, not randomness. Most were from one company so results may not generalize across organizations, especially small or non-software focused companies. Surveys rely on self-report. Comparisons between domains used slightly different wording for being technical. 

Overall, the study contributes a contextualized framework of engineering management attributes, evidence of differences between software engineering and other knowledge work, and implications for research and practice on measuring and developing management skills in software engineering.

Section 6: 

In conclusion, the key contributions are:

- A framework of 15 attributes for great engineering managers, with examples of strategies to demonstrate them. This can inform research on measuring management's impact and help managers know which skills to develop.

- Empirical evidence showing the similarities and differences in perceptions between software engineering and other knowledge work domains. This is the first study to investigate this empirically.

- Findings on the role of technical skills versus people management skills, which can guide manager selection and training processes tailored to software engineering.

The results have implications for research and practice. The attributes relate to concepts in management psychology, providing theories to test for further studies. The tailored framework can guide managers on skills to focus on and help create management training specific to software engineering needs.

Overall the study offers a timely exploration of engineering management in practice. It highlights relevant attributes through a rigorous qualitative and quantitative approach. The contextualized attributes build understanding of how managers impact the software engineering process and teams.

## Action Items 

- Review the framework of engineering manager attributes and assess your strengths and development areas as a manager. Identify 2-3 attributes to focus your learning and growth on.

- Discuss with your team how you currently demonstrate the attributes of a great engineering manager. Get their confidential input on your strengths and areas for improvement.

- If involved in hiring engineering managers, update job descriptions and interview practices to focus more on assessing people management skills rather than only technical skills.

- Advocate for increased management training at your organization, customized to the software engineering context based on the attributes framework. Volunteer to help design the curriculum. 

- Set up a coaching or mentorship program for new and existing engineering managers to develop their "soft" skills using the attributes as a guide.

- Organize a team discussion on expectations of engineering managers. Review the aligned and differing opinions between roles found in the study. 

- Compare employee satisfaction for managers with stronger people management skills versus technical skills. Use the data to guide your management development programs.

- Measure engineering productivity for teams with managers exhibiting certain attributes versus others. Use the data to identify effective management behaviors.

- Survey your software engineers and engineering managers to assess views on management attributes. Compare results to the study findings and tailor manager development accordingly.

## Quiet Quitting: Employees Respond to Poor Leadership

Quiet quitting means employees only do the minimum required at work. They don't give extra effort beyond their job duties. The authors looked at data on over 13,000 employees rating their managers. They found quiet quitting happens more with bad managers. 

With the best managers, 62% of employees give extra effort. Only 3% quietly quit. But with the worst managers, just 20% give extra effort. And 14% quietly quit. So bad management leads to 3-4 times more quiet quitting.  

Usually, quiet quitting is the employee's reaction to bad leadership. They feel undervalued, so they do the bare minimum. Most people have had a boss that made them want to quit. They also likely had a boss who inspired extra effort without resentment.

If you manage quiet quitters, look at your own leadership. Don't just blame the employees. Make sure you show employees they are valued. Have open and honest talks about expectations. Build trust by having good relationships with all employees. Find common interests and respect differences like age, gender or politics. Deliver consistently on promises. And build expertise so they trust your advice.

With the best leaders, few employees quietly quit. But even the best have around 3-4% who do. If it's just one employee, it may not be your fault. But try improving how you balance results with valuing your team. Don't just pressure for results. Make sure employees feel cared about. 

Blaming quiet quitting on lazy workers is wrong. This data shows it's usually about bad management instead. Employees want to bring energy, creativity and enthusiasm to companies and leaders that deserve it. Good leaders inspire extra effort without pressure. Bad ones cause quiet quitting.

## Action Items 


- Reflect on your own leadership and relationships with employees. Don't automatically blame them if there is quiet quitting.

- Have open conversations with employees about expectations and make sure they feel valued.

- Build trust and stronger relationships by finding common interests, respecting differences, delivering consistently, and building expertise. 

- Make sure you balance a focus on results with showing concern for employees' needs. Don't just pressure for results.

- Ask employees directly if there are things you can improve to inspire more discretionary effort from them. Listen non-defensively. 

- Work on developing skills as a motivational leader that brings out the best in people. Get training if needed.

- Evaluate if inappropriate behavior, bias or lack of inclusion on your part is driving quiet quitting and improve.

- If an employee still quietly quits after relationship improvements, accept it may not be your fault and focus on those you can motivate.

- Advocate for better leadership training and development in your organization if management issues seem widespread.

- Regularly self-reflect and get 360 reviews to improve your balance of driving results and supporting employees.

- Make sure overworked employees get rest and support so they don't burn out. Encourage work-life balance.
