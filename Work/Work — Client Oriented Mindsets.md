## Six Advisor Mindsets that Change Everything

### Introduction

Corporate training often emphasizes knowledge, skill improvement, and behavioral change. However, the most significant impact on performance and success can come from altering one's mindset. Mindsets shape our outlook and can either hold us back or empower us to achieve great things. This essay explores six transformative mindsets that define successful trusted advisors and highlights their key takeaways.



### Section 1: The Independent Mindset

The first critical mindset for trusted advisors is independence. Great advisors are devoted to their clients, but they also maintain their autonomy. Intellectual independence enables them to disagree and say "No" when necessary. Emotional independence ensures that insecurities and neediness do not interfere with objective advice. Financial independence ensures that financial gains do not compromise recommendations. By cultivating these types of independence, advisors can provide genuine and trustworthy guidance to their clients.



**Key Takeaways:**
1. Embrace intellectual, emotional, and financial independence in client relationships.
2. Prioritize clients' best interests over personal gain.



### Section 2: The Possibility Mindset

Successful trusted advisors possess a Possibility Mindset, always seeking opportunities and growth for their clients. They maintain a positive and optimistic demeanor, believing in the abundance of rewards for everyone involved. In contrast, advisors with a scarcity mentality focus on risks and are concerned with getting their fair share. Possibility-minded advisors are willing to invest in the short term to reap greater benefits later, while those with a scarcity mindset avoid long-term commitments.



**Key Takeaways:**
1. Foster a Possibility Mindset and focus on opportunities rather than limitations.
2. Exhibit positivity and vision in client interactions.



### Section 3: The Skeptical Mindset

While embracing possibilities, trusted advisors also maintain a healthy level of skepticism. They base their trust on observable behavior and data rather than mere words. Their motto is "trust but verify," ensuring they thoroughly investigate and fact-check clients' claims. This mindset enables them to make well-informed decisions and provide accurate advice.



**Key Takeaway:**
1. Develop healthy doubts and fact-check information to make informed decisions.



### Section 4: The Curiosity Mindset

Great advisors maintain a deep sense of curiosity, always asking questions to understand their clients' situations better. Unlike many adults, who lose their childlike curiosity, these advisors continually inquire, seeking to learn and understand more. Their passion for knowledge enables them to ask insightful questions and discover new insights that benefit their clients.



**Key Takeaway:**
1. Cultivate curiosity and never stop asking questions to gain a deeper understanding.



### Section 5: The Knowledge Mindset

The Knowledge Mindset emphasizes continuous learning and becoming a deep generalist. Trusted advisors understand that their success depends on having comprehensive knowledge in their field and beyond. They read widely and seek to broaden their knowledge base to better serve their clients' needs.

**Key Takeaway:**
1. Commit to lifelong learning and strive to be a knowledgeable expert.



### Section 6: The We Mindset

Successful advisors view their client relationships as collaborations. They work closely with their clients, engaging in give-and-take and freely sharing credit. In contrast, "experts for hire" seek control in the relationship, focusing on providing expertise as it is without collaboration. The "We Mindset" fosters strong partnerships and enables advisors to guide their clients toward achieving their most significant aspirations.

**Key Takeaways:**
1. Embrace a collaborative approach in client relationships.
2. Prioritize clients' goals and work together to achieve them.



### Conclusion

Changing one's mindset can profoundly impact performance and success as a trusted advisor. By adopting the six mindsets discussed—Independence, Possibility, Skepticism, Curiosity, Knowledge, and We—advisors can develop authentic and impactful relationships with their clients. These mindsets empower advisors to prioritize clients' interests, seek opportunities, remain objective, stay curious, continuously learn, and collaborate effectively. Ultimately, by nurturing these mindsets, the right knowledge, skills, and behaviors will naturally follow, leading to successful and fulfilling advisory careers.

Six Advisor Mindsets that Change Everything
Introduction
What makes a great advisor? Is it knowledge, skills, or behaviors? While these are important, they are not enough. The most fundamental impact on your performance and success comes from changing your mindset. Mindsets drive your outlook. They can bottle you up and restrict your freedom, or, they can free you to accomplish great things.

In this essay, I will describe six mindsets that characterize successful trusted advisors. These are:

•  The Independent Mindset

•  The Possibility Mindset

•  The Skeptical Mindset

•  The Curiosity Mindset

•  The Knowledge Mindset

•  The We Mindset

I will explain what each mindset means, why it is important, and how to cultivate it.

The Independent Mindset
Great advisors are devoted to their clients, but they are also independent. In my first book, Clients for Life, I called this “Selfless Independence.” There are three types of independence you need to cultivate. First, intellectual independence, where you’re able to say “No” and disagree with your client when you feel they aren’t doing the right thing for themselves. Second, emotional independence, which means you don’t allow your insecurities and neediness to interfere with your objective advice. The third type is financial independence, where you don’t allow money—e.g., the prospect of earning a lot off of a sale—to influence your recommendations.

The Independent Mindset is important because it allows you to be honest, objective, and courageous with your clients. You can challenge them to think differently, to confront their assumptions, and to make better decisions. You can also avoid conflicts of interest and maintain your integrity.

To cultivate the Independent Mindset, you need to:

•  Develop a strong sense of self-worth and confidence in your value as an advisor.

•  Practice saying “No” and giving constructive feedback in a respectful way.

•  Set clear boundaries and expectations with your clients and yourself.

•  Be transparent about your fees and how they are determined.

The Possibility Mindset
The most successful trusted advisors see possibilities for their clients, not limitations. They:

•  Always look for opportunities, growth, and expansion.

•  Are positive and upbeat in their demeanor.

•  Feel that there are rewards enough to go around for everyone—they know that a “rising tide” lifts all boats.

•  Try to see the positive side of their client’s ideas and plans (while recognizing what may go wrong, of course).

•  Are willing to invest time and money in the short term in order to earn more later.

The Possibility Mindset is important because it inspires your clients to dream bigger, to take calculated risks, and to achieve more. It also makes you more attractive and enjoyable to work with. You become a source of energy and optimism for your clients.

To cultivate the Possibility Mindset, you need to:

•  Adopt a growth mindset rather than a fixed mindset. This means believing that you and your clients can learn new skills, overcome challenges, and improve with effort.

•  Surround yourself with positive people who support your vision and goals.

•  Celebrate your successes and those of your clients.

•  Seek out new experiences and perspectives that broaden your horizons.

The Skeptical Mindset
Trusted advisors have a Possibility Mindset but at the same time they are always a bit skeptical about what people tell them. They watch clients’ feet, not their mouths. They are convinced by observable behavior and data, not by mere words. Client advisors, metaphorically, all come from Missouri—the “Show Me” state. “Trust but verify” is their motto. When a client says, “We have great customer service,” your attitude should be, “That may be true. Let’s look more closely at that.” Have a doubting mind!

The Skeptical Mindset is important because it prevents you from being naive, gullible, or complacent. It helps you to avoid mistakes, biases, and blind spots. It also enables you to provide more accurate, reliable, and credible advice to your clients.

To cultivate the Skeptical Mindset, you need to:

•  Question everything. Don’t take anything for granted or at face value. Ask for evidence, examples, and explanations.

•  Do your own research. Don’t rely on hearsay or second-hand information. Verify facts and sources for yourself.

•  Think critically. Don’t accept arguments or claims without analyzing their logic, assumptions, and implications.

•  Seek feedback. Don’t assume that you know everything or that you are always right. Invite others to challenge your views and opinions.

The Curiosity Mindset
As we mature from children into adults, our curiosity shrinks. We learn about constraints, develop an aversion to “wrong” answers, and become smug in our expertise. We no longer ask, like a typical five-year old, “Why?” (When our son was five, he asked us, “Can bobcats eat glass?”—go figure!). Albert Einstein claimed that important elements of his theory of relativity had its origins in playful questions he used to daydream about as a teenager, such as “Could I ride on a light beam?”

Great advisors have a deep sense of curiosity. They are always asking their clients, “Can you tell me more about that?…Why is that so?…What’s causing this?…Can you explain that?” and so on.

The Curiosity Mindset is important because it drives you to learn more, to understand better, and to discover new insights. It also shows your clients that you are genuinely interested in them and their situation. It builds rapport and trust.

To cultivate the Curiosity Mindset, you need to:

•  Be humble. Don’t pretend that you know everything or that you have all the answers. Admit when you don’t know something and ask for help.

•  Be open-minded. Don’t judge or dismiss ideas or perspectives that are different from yours. Listen and learn from others.

•  Be playful. Don’t take yourself too seriously or be afraid to make mistakes. Experiment and have fun with your questions and hypotheses.

The Knowledge Mindset
David Ogilvy, the advertising great, has a knowledge mindset—he loved to learn and based his create strategies on solid business facts: whereas many advertising professionals relied on “creative instinct” alone to develop new ideas, Ogilvy believed in carrying out in-depth research about every aspect of a company’s products, customers, and competitors. In his classic book, Ogilvy on Advertising, in a section entitled “Pursuit of Knowledge,” he writes: “I once asked Sir Hugh Rigby, surgeon to King George V, ‘What makes a great surgeon?’ Sir Hugh responded, ‘There isn’t much to choose between surgeons in manual dexterity. What distinguishes the great surgeon is that he knows more than other surgeons.’”

I believe it is the same with trusted advisors: The good ones know more.

Echoing David Ogilvy’s observation, leadership authority Warren Bennis told me once, “The professionals who develop into really great advisors are deep generalists. They develop a unique blend of knowledge depth and knowledge breadth.” This means reading widely—and outside their professional field.

The Knowledge Mindset is important because it enables you to provide more value, relevance, and impact to your clients. It also enhances your credibility and authority as an advisor. You can offer more insights, solutions, and perspectives to your clients.

To cultivate the Knowledge Mindset, you need to:

•  Be curious. See above.

•  Be diligent. Don’t settle for superficial or outdated information. Dig deeper and keep yourself updated on the latest trends and developments.

•  Be diverse. Don’t limit yourself to one domain or discipline. Explore different fields and topics that relate to your client’s business or industry.

The We Mindset
Trusted advisors view their work as a collaboration with their clients. Their goal is to help their clients shape and achieve their most important aspirations. This means collaborating—working closely together, engaging in give-and-take, and freely giving credit to the other person. In contrast, the typical “expert for hire” wants to have control in the relationship—their mindset frames their role as providing expertise that the client must use, “as is.”

The We Mindset is important because it fosters a partnership with your clients based on mutual respect, trust, and alignment. It also creates a sense of ownership and accountability for the outcomes of your work. You and your client become a team that works towards a common vision and goal.

To cultivate the We Mindset, you need to:

•  Be empathetic. Don’t focus only on your own agenda or perspective. Try to understand your client’s needs, goals, challenges, and emotions.

•  Be supportive. Don’t criticize or blame your client for their mistakes or shortcomings. Help them overcome their obstacles and celebrate their achievements.

•  Be inclusive. Don’t exclude or ignore your client’s input or feedback. Involve them in every stage of your work and acknowledge their contributions.

Conclusion
In this essay, I have described six mindsets that characterize successful trusted advisors:

•  The Independent Mindset

•  The Possibility Mindset

•  The Skeptical Mindset

•  The Curiosity Mindset

•  The Knowledge Mindset

•  The We Mindset

These mindsets drive your outlook and behavior as an advisor. They can help you to be more effective, influential, and valuable to your clients.

To develop these mindsets, you need to be aware of your current attitudes and beliefs, challenge them when they are limiting or negative, and adopt new ones that are empowering and positive.

By changing your mindsets, you can change everything.
