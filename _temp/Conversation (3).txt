Sure, I can explain the difference between sachou, danchou, and kouchou. They are all Japanese words that end with the kanji 長, which means long, leader, superior, or senior. However, they have different meanings and functions depending on the first kanji.
 - Sachou (社長) means company president, manager, or director. It is the highest position in a company, and the person who is in charge of the whole business. A sachou is usually elected by the board of directors or shareholders of the company.
 - Danchou (団長) means leader of a delegation, body, or party . It is a position that represents a group of people who have a common purpose or interest, such as a sports team, a musical band, a political party, or a diplomatic mission. A danchou is usually chosen by the members of the group or appointed by an authority.
 - Kouchou (校長) means principal or headmaster. It is the highest position in a school, and the person who is responsible for the education and administration of the students and teachers. A kouchou is usually hired by the board of education or the school district.

A team leader is usually called chīmurīdā (チームリーダー) in Japanese. This is a loanword from English, and it is written in katakana. It can be used for any kind of team, such as a sports team, a project team, or a work team.
Another possible word for team leader is hancho (班長), which means leader of a squad or group. This is a native Japanese word, and it is written in kanji. It is often used for teams within a company or an organization, such as a production team, a quality control team, or a maintenance team.
Both words are usually followed by the honorific suffix -san (さん) to show respect, unless you are very close or familiar with the person. For example:
 - Yamada-san wa chīmurīdā desu. (山田さんはチームリーダーです。) Yamada is the team leader.
 - Suzuki-san wa hancho desu. (鈴木さんは班長です。) Suzuki is the squad leader.
